Widows & Orphans

✗ Widow

Typography is the art and technique of arranging type to make written language legible, readable, and appealing when well set.

A widow is a short word stranded alone at the end of a paragraph.

✗ Orphan

Typography is the art of arranging type to make written language readable and visually appealing.

An orphan is the first line of a paragraph isolated at the bottom of a column.

Flush Left / Ragged Right

Typography is the art of arranging type to make written language legible and appealing. The right edge varies naturally.

Most natural for Western reading. Best choice for body text in almost any context.

Flush Right / Ragged Left

Typography is the art of arranging type to make written language legible and appealing. The left edge is uneven.

Creates visual tension. Best used for captions, pull quotes, or sidebar content.

Centered

Typography is the art of arranging type to make written language legible and appealing. Both edges are ragged.

Elegant for headlines, invitations, and short display text. Hard for long passages.

Justified

Typography is the art of arranging type to make written language legible, readable, and appealing when columns are properly set.

Both edges align to margins. Common in books. Can create "rivers" of space if poorly set.

Serif and sans-serif are the two foundational categories of typefaces. Understanding their anatomy, personality, and purpose is essential to making confident typographic decisions.

Serif Classic
Rg
Small strokes (serifs) finish the ends of letterforms
Varied stroke width — thick and thin transitions
Feels: authoritative, elegant, trustworthy, traditional
The serif: A small decorative foot or stroke at the end of a main stroke. In "R", notice the feet at the bottom of each leg — those are serifs. They were inherited from Roman stone carving traditions over 2,000 years ago.
Sans-Serif Modern
Rg
"Sans" means "without" in French — no finishing strokes
Uniform stroke width — consistent, geometric forms
Feels: modern, clean, approachable, minimal, digital
Clean terminals: Sans-serif letters end with a flat or slightly angled cut — no extra decoration. Born from the industrial revolution, popularized by the Bauhaus movement as a symbol of modernity and functionalism.

When to Use Each
📚
Books & Long Reading
Serifs guide the eye horizontally, reducing fatigue over long paragraphs. Print books have used serifs for 500+ years.
Serif
📱
Screens & Interfaces
Sans-serifs render more cleanly at small sizes on screens. They dominate UI design, apps, and websites for good reason.
Sans-Serif
🏛️
Law & Finance
Serifs signal tradition, authority and trustworthiness. Legal documents, formal letterheads and banks lean heavily on them.
Serif
🚀
Tech & Startups
Sans-serifs project forward-thinking, innovation and accessibility. The default choice for tech products and modern brands.
Sans-Serif
🗞️
Editorial & News
Publications mix both — serif headlines with sans-serif captions, or vice versa. Pairing creates visual hierarchy.
Both
🎨
Luxury & Fashion
High-contrast serifs (Didot, Bodoni) or extremely refined geometric sans. Both signal exclusivity when used at scale.
Both

The Four Pillars of Typographic Emotion
⚖️
Weight
Heavy weights project authority and urgency. Light weights convey delicacy or vulnerability.
↔️
Space
Wide tracking creates distance and isolation. Tight tracking builds tension and energy.
◯
Shape
Round forms feel warm and approachable. Angular forms suggest precision or aggression.
♩
Rhythm
The pace letterforms move across a line creates musical cadence — frantic, mournful, or soaring.
